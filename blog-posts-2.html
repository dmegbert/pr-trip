Biobay - La Parguera, Puerto Rico

The bioluminescent bay in La Parguera, Puerto Rico, glows not because of human chemicals, but because of the chemicals that dinoflagellate let off when agitated.

Now you’re probably wondering, “What are dinoflagellates?” Dinoflagellate are microorganisms that live in bio bays. Like the one in La Parguera. They let off the chemicals when agitated. They are actually a type of algae. Current estimates says that there are a total of 2,294 living species of dinoflagellate. When you go swimming in the bio bay, (which is salty, although over 220 species are from fresh-water areas) you alarm the dinoflagellate, causing them to give off the bioluminescent chemicals.

Note that if a bloom of dinoflagellates is found--it’s called a red tide. Now if you happen to eat a shellfish that's been contaminated by the red tide, you’ll get shellfish poisoning. But hopefully you’re just swimming in the glowing waters, not eating the creatures that live there.

Back to the bay. There are 3 bio-bays in Puerto Rico, Mosquito Bay in Vieques, Laguna Grande in the north-east in Fajardo, and La Parguera in the south-west in Lajas. Another cool thing about the bay is that you can see the stars very clearly, and the moon seems to be different colors, red, green, and I think, blue. The darker the moon color, the brighter the bay.

So, the question you all have been waiting to ask, is it worth it?

The answer is, it depends on what kind of boat you have. If it’s this huge ship then no! It’s not worth it. Why? Because you won’t be able to swim. If you get a smaller, more private boat, then yeah, it’ll be worth it because you’ll be able to swim. Speaking of swimming, since the water is so salty, you don’t have to try that hard to float. Now, it’s not as salty as the dead sea, or as salty as me after I lose a soccer game to another team, but still, it’s salty.
